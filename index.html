What Factors Are Most Associated with Heart Disease Risk?

An interactive data visualization project exploring how age, gender, chest pain type, and clinical measurements relate to heart disease outcomes.

Overview
Why Heart Disease?

Heart disease is one of the leading causes of death worldwide, making it a critical public health concern. Understanding the factors associated with it can help reduce preventable deaths and improve overall health outcomes. Identifying key risk factors supports earlier detection, more effective prevention, and better-informed healthcare decisions.

In this project, we explore how different demographic and health-related variables — including age, biological characteristics, and lifestyle indicators such as cholesterol levels and blood pressure — relate to the likelihood of developing heart disease. Through interactive data visualization, we transform complex medical data into intuitive insights that are easier to interpret and communicate.

Studying heart disease connects data analysis with real-world impact. Patterns in the data can reveal disparities across different groups and highlight potential areas for intervention.

Dataset
About the Data

Our analysis uses a publicly available heart disease dataset from Kaggle containing approximately 270,000 observations and 14 features. We apply stratified sampling by disease status (n=8,000) to preserve the original class distribution while ensuring efficiency in visualization.

Age
Age of the individual
Sex
Gender of the individual
Chest_Pain_Type
Type of chest pain experienced
Resting_BP
Resting blood pressure
Cholesterol
Serum cholesterol level
Fasting_Blood_Sugar
Whether fasting blood sugar exceeds threshold
Rest_ECG
Resting ECG results
Max_Heart_Rate
Maximum heart rate achieved
Exercise_Induced_Angina
Presence of exercise-induced angina
ST_Depression
ST depression induced by exercise
Heart_Disease
🎯 Target variable — presence of heart disease
Visualizations
Five Ways to See the Data
1
Altair · Histogram
Age Distribution by Heart Disease Status
Goal: Show how heart disease prevalence varies with age across the population.
💡 How to use: Hover over any bar to see the disease status and count for that age bin. Scroll to zoom into a specific age range.

Takeaway

Heart disease cases become increasingly concentrated in older age bins, particularly from the mid-50s onward. While younger individuals do appear in the disease group, the proportion rises noticeably with age. The overlap across all ages shows that age alone is not sufficient to predict heart disease.

Design Choices

Age is mapped to the x-axis and count to the y-axis. Transparent overlapping bars let both groups be compared simultaneously. 25 bins balance granularity and readability. Color consistently encodes disease status across all charts.

🖱 Hover — count & disease status 🔍 Scroll to zoom
2
D3.js · Interactive Heatmap
Heart Disease Rate by Sex and Chest Pain Type
Goal: Reveal how combinations of sex, chest pain type, and age group together shape heart disease risk.
💡 How to use: Use the Age Group dropdown to filter the heatmap. Hover over any cell to see the exact disease rate and count. Click a cell to highlight it. Darker red means higher disease rate.

Takeaway

Heart disease risk is shaped by combinations of factors. Males with asymptomatic chest pain have a dramatically higher disease rate compared to females with the same pain type. Filtering to 60+ shows elevated rates nearly everywhere, while the under-50 group shows much lower and more uniform rates across all combinations.

Design Choices

A red sequential color scale encodes disease rate intuitively — darker means more dangerous. Percentage labels inside cells make values immediately readable without hovering. The age group dropdown lets users explore how risk patterns shift across life stages.

📋 Dropdown — All / <50 / 50–60 / 60+ 🖱 Hover tooltip — sex, pain type, rate, count 🔲 Click to highlight cell
3
Altair · Bar Chart
Heart Disease Rate by Chest Pain Type
Goal: Compare heart disease risk across chest pain categories, filterable by sex.
💡 How to use: Use the Sex dropdown at the top of the chart to filter by All, male, or female. Hover over bars to see the exact disease rate and total count.

Takeaway

Asymptomatic chest pain has a strikingly higher heart disease rate — a counterintuitive but clinically significant finding. When filtering by sex, males show much higher rates across all pain types. Female patterns differ, suggesting the symptom-disease relationship varies by sex.

Design Choices

Disease rate rather than raw count is used on the y-axis for fair comparison across groups of different sizes. Bars are sorted highest to lowest. Color encodes chest pain type for quick visual differentiation.

📋 Dropdown — All / Male / Female 🖱 Hover — rate & count
4
Altair · Scatter Plot
Max Heart Rate vs ST Depression by Disease Status
Goal: Explore the joint relationship between two clinical measurements and disease status.
💡 How to use: Hover over any point to see detailed patient info including age, sex, max heart rate, and ST depression. Click and drag on the chart to brush-select a region — selected points stay colored while others fade to gray.

Takeaway

Disease cases cluster at higher ST depression values and lower maximum heart rates. Separate regression lines confirm the relationship between these variables differs by disease status — the disease group shows higher ST depression across the range of heart rates.

Design Choices

Partial transparency prevents overplotting on dense regions. Regression lines summarize each group's trend without hiding individual points. Brush selection enables focused exploration of specific clusters.

🖱 Hover — age, sex, max HR, ST depression ✏️ Drag to brush-select region
5
Altair · Box Plot
Distribution of Clinical Variables by Disease Status
Goal: Compare the spread and median of clinical variables across disease groups.
💡 How to use: Use the Variable dropdown at the top of the chart to switch between Cholesterol, Max Heart Rate, and ST Depression. Hover over each box to see the median and quartile values for each group.

Takeaway

ST depression shows the clearest separation between groups with minimal overlap, making it a strong individual predictor. The disease group also tends to have lower maximum heart rates. Cholesterol distributions overlap heavily, suggesting it is a weaker standalone predictor.

Design Choices

A dropdown replaces three separate charts, keeping the layout clean while enabling direct comparison. Box size is standardized so visual weight stays consistent. Color matches the rest of the project for disease status.

📋 Dropdown — Cholesterol / Max HR / ST Depression 🖱 Hover — median & quartile values
Conclusion
What We Learned

Across all five visualizations, consistent patterns emerge. Heart disease risk is not driven by any single variable but by combinations of demographic and clinical factors. The most meaningful associations involve asymptomatic chest pain, elevated ST depression, lower maximum heart rates, male sex, and older age — especially when these occur together.

📈

Age Increases Risk

Disease cases concentrate heavily in individuals in their 50s and 60s. Younger groups show substantially lower and more uniform rates.

⚠️

Asymptomatic Pain is Dangerous

Patients with asymptomatic chest pain had the highest disease rates — a counterintuitive finding that highlights a major screening challenge.

⚤

Sex Modifies Risk

Males show dramatically higher rates across most chest pain types. The sex and pain type combination is more predictive than either variable alone.

💓

ST Depression is Key

ST depression shows the clearest group separation with minimal distributional overlap — stronger than cholesterol or max heart rate alone.

🔬

Cholesterol is Weaker Alone

Cholesterol distributions overlap heavily between groups, suggesting it operates more meaningfully in combination with other risk factors.

🔮

Future Work

Future directions include predictive modeling, interaction effect analysis, and exploration of underrepresented subgroups in the data.
